PHYSICAL CUE (user-supplied, high-yield): the seabed seen THROUGH water must be DARKENED, not lightened -- wet/submerged sand is markedly darker than its dry albedo. In a refraction water model where the refracted ground colour (refrCol from the scene-copy tex) is the base, multiply it down hard (~0.4) before the absorption/scatter mix: waterBody = refrCol*0.40*absorb + deepColor*(1-absorb). This is what finally made shallow water read TRANSPARENT-yet-WET instead of a pale milky film: a bright unmodified seabed under a thin scatter veil always reads milky/light no matter how the veil is tuned, because the eye expects submerged ground to be dark. Darken the ground first, THEN tint. General lesson: when a 'see-through' material reads milky despite a correct transparency/scatter model, check the BRIGHTNESS of the thing seen through it -- real media (water, glass, ice) attenuate transmitted light, so the background must be dimmed, not passed at full albedo.
